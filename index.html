Documentation for Dead Roads

By Nathan Wang and Bill Qin

Week 1 (3/19 - 3/24)

Nathan:
I spent this week mostly watching. I brainstormed with Bill for a bit about what we wanted to do, and we started making a list of the stuff we wanted to be in our film. Eventually, we will cut down the wish list to a list of things that we can actually afford to put into our film (with time and resource constraints and all). The list is in the ’Brainstorming’ document in our shared folder. I watched a few 20 minute silent films, and those were very interesting because of how much the actors had to pantomime to get their points across. They were surprisingly effective, and I have a weird newfound respect for silent films now. I also watched parts of Enter the Dragon (with Bruce Lee), since I wanted some inspiration for an action sequence. I wasn’t really a fan of how there wasn’t much buildup in the fight scenes, and how Bruce Lee almost always instantly won. If Bill and I were to do an action scene, I would probably go for more of an even fight with more motives.

Bill:
This week, I focused on one film in particular, Ikiru. While not filled with action shots, this Kurosawa film focused on a man who was diagnosed with terminal cancer who tries to find meaning in the numbered days he has left. I was able to find some interesting shots that I thought could be implemented into our own film as well as those I found to just be all-around effective. Shots that use a mirror to be able to see front-facing views on both characters while they are directly facing each other and the use of a jungle gym as a prison proves incredibly effective and something we can aspire to have in our own film. Along with Nathan, we also watched some silent films and gained an appreciation for expression without words (some of the films went a little far to a point of comedy, and that’s not for us, but the point is still there). As Nathan stated, we spent some time brainstorming on ideas and coming up with a general direction. We have already expressed interest in logistics of our film (such as having an action scene and focusing less on dialogue) and feel confident in our ability to compose a fully-fledged proposal by Friday.

Week 2 (3/25 - 3/31)

Bill:
This week, Nathan and I have developed the framework for how we want our project to look like, including pinpointing certain scenes we want to try and implement into our film. Challenges such as giving the impression of a moving car without having to drive ourselves, telling plot and emotion through movement and not dialogue, and the usage of more advanced film techniques such as shutter speed and aperture to get the exact shot we want. I have further immersed myself in watching film. Knowing certain shots I want, it’s been easier to pinpoint screen captures that I know I can draw inspiration from (see “Better Luck Tomorrow“), but I also pay attention to shots I find good overall.

Nathan:
It’s been a productive week. We settled on a good idea and we have a very clear vision of what we want certain key shots to be like. Like Bill said above, we got quite a few good ideas for shots in the car. I’m thinking of taking footage from the same camera angle and overlaying them to get the “moving“ effect (replace Mr. Kelman moving with Bill pretending to be driving, but keeping the windows moving)

Week 3 (4/1 - 4/7)

Bill
This week was mostly about visualizing the plot and making preparations for our first shoot. We’ve been considering actors and their schedules, places to film, and anticipating obstacles we may encounter in our first shoot while coming up with solutions now. One of the biggest obstacles we foresaw was giving the impression of a moving car from the inside without needing an actor to actually drive the car. To do this, we decided to film someone else (breaking the persona, but that’ll be you, Mr. Kelman) driving the car and using the actual motion of the car (window movement, sound of car moving, etc.) to hide the actual stillness. Another thing we discussed was how this scene would be shot at nighttime and how that would call for a change in the way our camera functions. We tested a bit with the aperture and shutter speed to find the settings that made sense for us on an outside road that likely would have artificial lighting from lamp posts or house light. We have yet to test this with the inside of a car when it’s night outside.

Nathan
I’ve been thinking about how serious this film could be. We could take two approaches: One is a lighthearted film capitalizing on the humor that we can draw from different characters (the narcissistic drunk driver for example). Or we could take a different approach and have a film with minimal dialogue, with a bunch of close up shots and clear sound effects telling the story. The plot still needs some hammering down near the beginning and the end, but I think that we’re making good progress. We aim to begin filming by next weekend.
